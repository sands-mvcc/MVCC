Module 1

Foundations of Data

Before we analyze anything, we need to understand what data is and how it's collected correctly.

📖 Step 1 — What is Data?

Data is any collected observation or measurement about the world. Think of it as recorded evidence.

Analogy: Imagine you're a detective. Data is every clue you collect at a crime scene — footprints, fingerprints, witness statements. Statistics is the process of making sense of all those clues.

Data comes in two fundamental types:

Quantitative (Numbers)
Qualitative (Categories)

Quantitative data is numeric and measurable.

Sub-typeDefinitionExample
DiscreteCountable, whole numbersNumber of students: 27
ContinuousAny value in a rangeTemperature: 98.6°F

Qualitative data describes categories or labels.

Sub-typeDefinitionExample
NominalNo natural orderEye color: Blue, Brown, Green
OrdinalHas a natural orderRating: Poor / Fair / Good / Excellent

📋 Step 2 — Population vs. Sample

1

Population

The entire group you want to study. Example: all 330 million Americans.

2

Sample

A subset of the population we actually measure. Example: 1,500 surveyed Americans.

3

Why Sample?

Measuring every member of a large population is often impossible or impractical. A well-chosen sample lets us infer facts about the whole population.

⚠️ Key Risk — Sampling Bias: If a sample isn't representative, conclusions will be wrong. Example: polling only smartphone users about internet access skews results.

Module 2

Descriptive Statistics

Summarizing and describing data with numbers — the foundation of all analysis.

📐 Step 1 — Measures of Center

These tell you where data clusters — the "typical" value.

1

Mean (Average)

Add all values, divide by count. Most sensitive to extreme values (outliers).

Formula — Population Mean
μ = (Σxᵢ) / N
μ = mean  |  Σ = sum of all values  |  N = number of values
2

Median

The middle value when data is sorted. Resistant to outliers — great for skewed data like income.

How to Find Median
Sort data → pick middle value (or avg of two middle values)
Example: [2, 5, 7, 9, 11] → Median = 7
3

Mode

The most frequently occurring value. Can have multiple modes (bimodal, multimodal). Useful for categorical data.

Example
[3, 5, 5, 7, 9, 5, 2] → Mode = 5
5 appears 3 times — more than any other value
When to Use Which?
• Mean: Symmetric data with no extreme outliers (heights, test scores)
• Median: Skewed data or outliers present (income, house prices)
• Mode: Categorical data or finding most popular option

📏 Step 2 — Measures of Spread

Center alone isn't enough. Two datasets can have the same mean but look completely different. Spread tells us how much the data varies.

1

Range

Simplest measure: Max − Min. Very sensitive to outliers.

2

Variance (σ²)

Average of the squared deviations from the mean. Squaring ensures negatives don't cancel out positives.

Population Variance
σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N
Each value's distance from the mean is squared, then averaged
Sample Variance (uses N−1 to correct for bias)
s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1)
N−1 = "Bessel's correction" — makes the sample estimate unbiased
3

Standard Deviation (σ or s)

Square root of variance. Returns the spread to the same units as the data — much more interpretable.

Standard Deviation
σ = √(σ²)     s = √(s²)
If σ = 5 points on a test, most scores fall within ±5 of the mean
The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7 Rule):
For bell-shaped (normal) distributions:
• 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean
• 95% within 2 standard deviations
• 99.7% within 3 standard deviations

Module 3

Probability Theory

Probability is the mathematical language of uncertainty — it quantifies how likely events are to occur.

🎲 Step 1 — What is Probability?

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 that measures how likely an event is:

Basic Probability
P(A) = (Number of favorable outcomes) / (Total possible outcomes)
Example: Rolling a 3 on a fair die → P(3) = 1/6 ≈ 0.167
The Scale:
P = 0 → Impossible  |  P = 0.5 → 50/50  |  P = 1 → Certain

Visualized: 20 outcomes — how many are "red"?

📜 Step 2 — Probability Rules

1

Complement Rule

The probability an event does NOT happen equals 1 minus the probability it does.

P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A)
P(not raining) = 1 − P(raining) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7
2

Addition Rule (OR)

Probability that event A or B occurs:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
Subtract overlap to avoid double-counting. If mutually exclusive: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
3

Multiplication Rule (AND)

Probability that both A and B occur:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
If A and B are independent: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
4

Conditional Probability

Probability of A given we know B already happened:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
P(disease | positive test) — the basis of medical diagnostics

🔔 Step 3 — The Normal Distribution

The most important distribution in statistics. It's symmetric, bell-shaped, and described entirely by its mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ).

Why does the Normal Distribution appear everywhere?
The Central Limit Theorem (Module 4) explains that averages of large samples follow a normal distribution — regardless of the original data's shape.

Module 4

Sampling Distributions

The bridge between a sample and conclusions about the population.

🔔 Step 1 — What is a Sampling Distribution?

Analogy: Imagine you're estimating the average height of all adults in a city. You take many random samples of 30 people each and calculate the mean height for each sample. The distribution of all those sample means is the sampling distribution of the mean.
1

Take many random samples from a population

Each sample has its own mean (x̄).

2

Plot the distribution of those sample means

This creates the sampling distribution.

3

This distribution tells us how accurate our estimates are

It's the foundation of confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.

⚡ Step 2 — The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The most powerful theorem in statistics:

Central Limit Theorem: When you take large enough samples (n ≥ 30) from any population, the sampling distribution of the sample mean will be approximately normal, regardless of the population's shape.
Sampling Distribution of the Mean
x̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)
Sample means are normally distributed with mean μ and variance σ²/n

📉 Step 3 — Standard Error

The standard error (SE) is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution. It tells us how much sample means vary around the true population mean.

Standard Error of the Mean
SE = σ / √n
σ = population standard deviation  |  n = sample size
Key Insight: Larger samples → smaller standard error → more precise estimates.
Doubling the sample size cuts the standard error by √2 ≈ 1.41, not 2. To halve SE, you need 4× the data.
